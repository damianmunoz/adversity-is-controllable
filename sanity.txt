================================================================================
 1. THE BUG — what we found and why
================================================================================

Symptom
-------
Step 6 on ~244k features collapsed to:
    Final weights — WAIT: 0.0000  PASSIVE: 0.0000  AGGRESSIVE: 1.0000
Policy chose AGGRESSIVE 100% of the time — clearly wrong for any healthy
market-making / execution policy.

Root cause (two-layer)
----------------------
Shell inspection of the features parquet showed mid_price > best_ask for
~98% of rows. Walking back through the pipeline:

  Layer 1 (data):
    During early ingestion, the Binance WebSocket disconnected mid-stream.
    Depth updates were dropped during the outage. The local order book was
    never re-synced against a fresh REST snapshot, so many price levels
    carried stale quantities. Post-outage, best_bid and best_ask came from
    those stale levels while new updates landed elsewhere in the book.
    Net effect: a chronically misaligned book.

  Layer 2 (logic):
    `src/book/book_builder.py` on a sequence gap flagged `sequence_gap=True`
    only on the FIRST emission after the gap and then continued applying
    deltas as if the book were fine. Every subsequent snapshot was stamped
    `sequence_gap=False` even though the underlying book was corrupted.
    Filtering `sequence_gap=True` downstream removed one row — not the
    7+ hours of silent corruption that followed.

Not a bug in
  - Hedge algorithm
  - Kalman filter
  - Feature math
Pure garbage-in: features were physically impossible (mid outside [bid,ask]),
so Hedge rationally learned "PASSIVE fills look free, take them forever" →
collapsed to AGGRESSIVE because that was the only action whose loss signal
was remotely sensible in the broken data.

 5. CODE PATCHES — GAP SELF-HEAL
================================================================================

Goal: future overnight runs should automatically recover from WS disconnects
      instead of silently corrupting. Two files changed.

5.1  src/book/book_builder.py
-----------------------------
Old behavior:
  - Accepted a single snapshot_path
  - On gap: flagged sequence_gap=True on first emission, kept going

New behavior:
  - Accepts a snapshot_dir, loads all snapshots chronologically at run start
  - Holds snapshots[0] as seed, snapshots[1:] in reserve for recovery
  - On gap detected (first_update_id > last_update_id + 1):
      1. search remaining snapshots for one with
         last_update_id + 1 >= first_update_id  (i.e., bridges the gap)
      2. if found → reseed, set self.corrupted = False → continue cleanly
      3. if none  → set self.corrupted = True, keep applying deltas but emit
                   every subsequent snapshot with sequence_gap=True until
                   a fresh snapshot eventually appears
  - Output contract unchanged — existing filter downstream still works, and
    now sequence_gap=True is honest across the entire corrupted region.

New helper: `list_snapshots(snapshot_dir, symbol)` returns all snapshots in
chronological order (filename carries utc_ms).

5.2  src/ingest/ws_ingest.py
----------------------------
Added concurrent periodic REST snapshotting so the self-heal logic actually
has material to recover from.

  WSIngestConfig.snapshot_interval_sec = 900.0    # 15 min, configurable
  WSIngestConfig.symbol               = "BTCUSDT"

  async def snapshot_periodically(config):

================================================================================
 9. λ (LAMBDA) — RETUNED FROM THE LOSS COMPONENT DISTRIBUTIONS
================================================================================

Observation from the execution log
----------------------------------
Across all 38,972 ticks:
  |mean(slippage)|    = 0.0013      std(slippage)     = 0.0247
   mean(adverse_move) = 0.4750      std(adverse_move) = 2.3022

Ratio at the means:
   λ * mean(adverse) / |mean(slippage)|   at λ=0.5  =  ~190x

At λ=0.5, the adverse-move term was roughly 190x larger than the slippage
term at their means. Since adverse_move is largely noise (random future
walks), this meant Hedge was effectively learning from noise, not from
execution quality.

Principled λ reference points from the data
-------------------------------------------
  λ = 0.0027   |mean(s)| / mean(a)       — balances signal contribution
  λ = 0.0107   std(s)    / std(a)        — balances noise contribution
  λ = 0.5      (previous default)
  λ = 1.0      treats timing & slippage 1:1 in absolute $ terms

Equilibrium expected loss per action at each λ
----------------------------------------------
  λ       WAIT       PASSIVE    AGGR       best
  0.00    0.0000    -0.0047     0.0055     PASSIVE
  0.10    0.0402     0.0751     0.0440     WAIT
  0.25    0.1004     0.1948     0.1017     WAIT
  0.50    0.2008     0.3943     0.1979     AGGRESSIVE
  1.00    0.4016     0.7933     0.3903     AGGRESSIVE
  2.00    0.8031     1.5914     0.7751     AGGRESSIVE

At λ ≥ 0.5, adverse-move noise overwhelms slippage and AGGRESSIVE looks
better in aggregate simply because it doesn't fail-to-fill. The policy
cannot learn regime-conditioned behaviour there — the best action is
nearly constant.

Decision — BEFORE / AFTER
-------------------------
  BEFORE: lambda_ = 0.5
  AFTER:  lambda_ = 0.1

0.1 sits between the std-equalized 0.011 and the previous default 0.5.
At 0.1, WAIT and AGGRESSIVE are nearly tied in expected loss (0.040 vs 0.044)
while PASSIVE is still penalised for adverse selection. This is the regime
where Hedge has to actually USE regime / market_pressure information to
decide when to be WAIT vs AGGRESSIVE — which is the research question.

Interpretation

  dropped independently. Not a λ artifact.

  less). Still healthy (range [-1.3, +2.1]), just less jittery.

  vol_5s itself is bounded by ~7e-4 in raw magnitude, and H[2,1]=1 pipes it
  directly into regime. Regime can only be as large as vol_5s regardless of
  how much the filter "trusts" the observation. Fix requires either scaling
  vol_5s to unit variance before Kalman, or choosing a different regime
  observation (vol_30s, or a log-vol). Deferred — not blocking.
- Final weights WAIT 0.44 / AGGR 0.40 / PASSIVE 0.16 is NOT policy

  other. WAIT and AGGR having near-equal total cost is a physical property
  of the data (WAIT saves spread but pays opportunity cost; AGGR pays spread
  but skips opportunity cost — they trade off).
- Action mix is FLAT across market_pressure quartiles (WAIT 39-41%, PASSIVE
  21-22%, AGGR 37-39%). This is evidence that vanilla Hedge is NOT using the
  Kalman state — which is expected: classical Hedge learns one single marginal
  distribution over actions, not state-conditioned.

Takeaway
--------
Execution quality improved materially. The policy is working correctly for
what it is (marginal Hedge). To actually exploit Kalman state we need a
state-conditioned policy — see §14.

14. FORWARD-LOOKING DECISIONS — two open design questions
================================================================================

14.A  Execution-log appending — is it a problem, and how to fix it
------------------------------------------------------------------

Current behavior
  scripts/6_simulate_and_update.py appends ExecutionRecord JSONL rows to
  data/derived/execution_log/date=*/log.jsonl. Each run adds more rows; the
  log never truncates. This made the retune-verification analysis painful
  (had to split a 77k-row file in half and infer which half came from which
  config by checking per-row λ).

Is it a problem?
  YES for a research project where every run is an experiment that should
  be comparable to other runs under different configs. NO for a production
  streaming system where the log is a single immutable event journal.
  We are research-stage, so: problem.

Options considered

  Option 1 — truncate log dir at start of every step-6 run
    Pro: simplest; always reflects current config.
    Con: destroys run history, can't diff configs, can't replay.
    Verdict: bad. Throws away information we need.

  Option 2 — overwrite with a --preserve flag for when you want history
    Pro: simple; opt-in preservation.
    Con: easy to forget the flag; still loses any baseline unintentionally.
    Verdict: fragile.

  Option 3 — run-ID partitioning of the output directory (RECOMMENDED)
    Each run writes to:
      data/derived/execution_log/run=YYYYMMDD-HHMMSS/date=*/log.jsonl
    Plus a `latest` symlink that always points at the most recent run.
    Pro: every run is self-contained and mutually diffable. Analysis scripts
         can iterate over runs for meta-analysis. Cleanup is `rm -rf run=…`.
         Mirrors how MLflow / W&B / any experiment tracker structures output.
         Zero risk of confusion between runs.
    Con: tiny bit more complex — need to record run_id, update scripts that
         read the log to resolve `latest`.
    Verdict: this is the long-term-right answer for a research project.

  Option 4 — add run_id as a column to each record, keep single append file
    Pro: no path restructure.
    Con: single file grows unboundedly; still need to filter on run_id for
         every analysis; dirties the record schema with experiment metadata.
    Verdict: worse than Option 3 and only marginally simpler.

Recommendation — Option 3 (run-ID partitioning) with a `latest` convenience
pointer. Near-minimal diff in updater.py (derive run_id from startup UTC ms,
insert it into the output path) + an optional rm/relink of `latest` symlink.
No downstream code needs to change if callers just resolve `latest` or scan
`run=*/`.


14.B  State-conditioned policy — is it in the project's interest?
-----------------------------------------------------------------

Project thesis restated:
  "Given current market conditions, how should we act to minimize execution
  cost and decision error?"

"Given current market conditions" is the key phrase. The Kalman filter's
whole purpose is to estimate those conditions as a hidden state. But the
current Hedge policy uses a SINGLE weight vector and picks actions from the
same distribution at every tick regardless of state. Evidence from §13:
action mix is flat across market_pressure quartiles. The Kalman output is
currently decorative.

So, is state-conditioned policy "in the project's interest"?
  YES — it is essential to the research thesis, not a nice-to-have.
  Without it, we only validate that Hedge converges to a reasonable marginal
  distribution, which is a trivial regret-minimization result. With it, we
  can answer the actual question: does knowing the estimated regime/pressure
  let the policy make better state-specific decisions than the marginal
  optimum?

Options for state-conditioned variant

  Option 1 — bucketed Hedge (RECOMMENDED as first step)
    Discretize market_pressure and regime into bins (e.g. 5 × 3 = 15 buckets),
    maintain one Hedge weight vector per bucket. At decision time, look up
    the current bucket from the Kalman state and sample from that bucket's
    weights. Update only that bucket's weights after observing loss.
    Pro: preserves classical Hedge regret guarantees PER BUCKET. Minimal
         math overhead. Directly tests the research thesis. Easy to compare
         to marginal Hedge on the same data.
    Con: data gets thinner per bucket (38k / 15 ≈ 2.6k ticks each in our
         current window), so noisier weight estimates. Fix: adaptive bucket
         boundaries based on quantiles so each bucket gets equal data.
    Verdict: the right first experiment.

  Option 2 — EXP4 / contextual Hedge with expert set
    Define an "expert" per state bucket (each expert recommends one fixed
    action in its bucket) and run Hedge over experts.
    Pro: cleaner theoretical framing; tighter regret bounds.
    Con: more complex to implement and tune. Diminishing returns over
         Option 1 for this scale of problem.
    Verdict: overkill for now; revisit if Option 1 shows promise.

  Option 3 — LinUCB / linear contextual bandit
    Model each action's expected loss as a linear function of the Kalman
    state. Pick the action with best optimistic estimate.
    Pro: handles continuous state directly; good sample complexity.
    Con: introduces UCB machinery and confidence-set math; departs from the
         "Hedge regret-minimization" spine of the project.
    Verdict: a different research project.

  Option 4 — keep marginal Hedge, add state to the loss function
    Not really state-conditioned. Doesn't answer the thesis question.
    Verdict: avoid.

Recommended order of operations
  1. FIRST fix the dead regime dimension (standardize vol_5s or pick a
     different regime observation). Without live regime, state-conditioned
     Hedge only has 1D (pressure) state to condition on, which partially
     tests the thesis but leaves the regime half of the claim untested.
  2. THEN implement bucketed Hedge (Option 1) as the state-conditioned
     variant. Keep the marginal Hedge code path as a baseline for A/B
     comparison on the same features.
  3. Report: bucketed total loss vs marginal total loss on identical data.
     If bucketed wins by >5%, the thesis holds empirically. If it doesn't,
     either the state estimates aren't informative enough, or optimal
     execution in BTC/USDT 1s data is near-constant across regimes — both
     are publishable findings.

16. WAKING UP THE DEAD REGIME DIMENSION (2026-04-22)
================================================================================

Written in plain language on purpose. The math is in src/state/kalman_filter.py
and configs/kalman.yaml if you want it.

WHAT WAS BROKEN
---------------
The Kalman filter tracks two hidden numbers at every tick:
  - market_pressure  : are people buying or selling harder right now?
  - regime           : how turbulent is the market right now?

market_pressure worked fine. regime was dead.

"Dead" means: no matter what the market did, regime barely moved.
Its standard deviation was about 0.00003. That is so small it might as
well be zero. The policy could not use it for anything.

WHY WAS IT DEAD
---------------
The filter got regime from one feature: vol_5s (volatility over the last
5 seconds).

Two problems with vol_5s:

  1. It's tiny.
     The biggest value we ever saw in 10.85h of data was 0.00071.
     The average was 0.00002.
     A tiny input can't produce a big output. Nothing we tweaked inside
     the filter (we tried — see §8) made regime any bigger, because the
     raw input itself was already that small.

  2. It's dead most of the time.
     Only 46 out of every 100 ticks had a nonzero vol_5s.
     Why? BTC price moves in $0.01 steps. In a 5-second window, the
     mid-price often doesn't move at all. No movement → log return = 0
     → volatility = 0. Lots of silence.

So regime was fed a tiny, silent signal. It produced a tiny, silent output.

THE FIX — THREE SIMPLE STEPS
----------------------------
Step 1. Switch from vol_5s to vol_30s.
  vol_30s is the same idea but over 30 seconds instead of 5.
  On the same data:
    vol_5s  was nonzero in 45.85% of ticks.
    vol_30s is nonzero in 91.47% of ticks.
  Twice as much signal. Much less silence.

Step 2. Rescale the number before the filter sees it.
  vol_30s is still tiny on an absolute scale (max ≈ 0.00031). So we
  "zoom in" on it by computing:

      rescaled = (vol_30s − average_vol_30s) / size_of_typical_vol_30s

  After this:
    - If vol_30s is exactly average, rescaled = 0.
    - If vol_30s is one typical step above average, rescaled = 1.
    - If two steps above, rescaled = 2. And so on.

  The filter sees values in the range a human would use, not numbers
  a millionth the size.

  The constants we measured on clean data:
      average_vol_30s   = 0.0000317
      typical step size = 0.0000271
  We hard-coded these into configs/kalman.yaml as obs_center and
  obs_scale. If the market's volatility character changes drastically
  in future data, these should be recomputed.

Step 3. Tell the filter the rescaled input has noise of size 1.
  Because we made the input unit-scale, its noise is also size 1 by
  construction. Set R[2,2] = 1.0 and the math just works.

17. SESSION-AWARE DATA LOADING IN SCRIPT 6 (2026-04-22)
================================================================================

WHAT WAS BROKEN
---------------
Script 6 used to run on ALL feature data in one shot. No way to restrict
it to a slice.

If two ingest sessions accidentally landed in the features folder
(picture: leave ingest running overnight, it hits a WS gap at 3am,
recovers at 3:05am, keeps going) script 6 would treat them as ONE long
stream. The Kalman filter's state at the start of the second session
would be whatever it was at the end of the first — an estimate of
market conditions from hours ago, applied to "now."

That's wrong. The Kalman filter is stateful. Its answer for tick t
depends on its memory of every tick before. If there's an actual gap,
that memory is stale.

WHAT IS A SESSION
-----------------
A session is an uninterrupted stream of ticks. No gaps. No WS
disconnects. No manual ingest restarts in the middle.

Calendar days DO NOT define sessions. Our current data starts at
19:54 UTC on 2026-04-20 and runs to 06:43 UTC the next day — that is
ONE session, even though it crosses midnight. Splitting it by calendar
date would chop it into two artificially.

What matters is: are consecutive ticks actually next to each other in time?

WHAT SCRIPT 6 DOES NOW
----------------------
At startup it:
  1. Loads every feature row from data/derived/features/.
  2. Walks through them. A new session begins at any of:
       a. the first row
       b. a row with sequence_gap=True
       c. a row whose timestamp is more than 5 SECONDS after the
          previous row
     (Normal ticks are ~1s, so 5s is generous — it won't false-positive
     on ordinary jitter, but it will catch any real interruption.)
  3. Prints a table of every session found: index, row count, duration
     in hours, start time, end time.
  4. Runs the pipeline on the LONGEST session by default.

CLI flags
---------
  python3 scripts/6_simulate_and_update.py
      → run on the longest detected session (safest default)

  python3 scripts/6_simulate_and_update.py --list-sessions
      → print the session table and exit, without running anything

  python3 scripts/6_simulate_and_update.py --session 2
      → pick session #2 from the table (1-indexed) instead of the longest

CURRENT STATE — WHAT YOU SEE RIGHT NOW
--------------------------------------
  Detected 1 session(s) in data/derived/features:
    idx  rows    hours   start                    end
    1    38973   10.83   2026-04-20 19:54:00 UTC  2026-04-21 06:43:32 UTC
  Running on session 1 — 38973 rows ...

One session because the clean 10.85h window has zero gaps. If future
overnight ingest runs introduce new gaps, the new sessions will show
up in this table automatically.

WHY "LONGEST" IS THE RIGHT DEFAULT
----------------------------------
- More data → more tick-by-tick updates → Hedge's regret bound tightens
  (the formal guarantee is O(√(T log K))).
- The longest contiguous session is always the cleanest big slice of
  data available. Picking shorter sessions by default would silently
  throw away signal.
- For generalization tests you WANT to pick non-default sessions so you
  can train on one and evaluate on another. That's why the flag exists.

WHAT DIDN'T CHANGE
------------------
- Kalman filter code is untouched by this change (except §16's rescaling).
- Policy code is untouched.
- The execution log format is unchanged (run-ID partitioned as before).
- Output behaviour for a single-session data directory is identical to
  the old behaviour — the script still processes every available tick.

19. NEW IDEA — 2D BUCKETING (PRESSURE × REGIME): WHAT, WHY, EXPECTED
================================================================================

Caveman brain version. The math is in src/policy/hedge.py if you want it.

THE QUESTION (still the same)
-----------------------------
"Given current market conditions, how should we act?"

Conditions = two numbers from the Kalman filter:
  market_pressure   : are people buying or selling harder right now?
  regime            : how turbulent is the market right now?

THE STORY SO FAR
----------------
- Marginal Hedge (the old one): IGNORES both numbers. Picks one action
  distribution and uses it everywhere. Like wearing the same shoes
  to the beach, the gym, and a wedding.

- 1D bucketed Hedge (§15): looks at PRESSURE only. Different shoes for
  different pressure. Beat marginal by ~29% on combined cost. Big win.

- regime was DEAD until §16. Couldn't bucket on it. Now alive.

WHAT WE'RE ADDING NOW (§19)
---------------------------
2D bucketed Hedge: looks at PRESSURE *AND* REGIME together. One set of
weights per (pressure_bucket, regime_bucket) pair.

THE ANALOGY
-----------
Imagine you're crossing a busy street.
  Pressure = "how many cars are coming."
  Regime   = "how fast they are moving."

A 1D-pressure-only policy says:
  "Lots of cars coming → wait. Few cars coming → walk."

A 2D policy says:
  "Lots of cars coming AND moving slow → walk anyway, you can dodge."
  "Lots of cars coming AND moving fast → wait, definitely wait."
  "Few cars coming AND moving slow → stroll."
  "Few cars coming AND moving fast → still walk, but quickly."

The 2D policy can give you a different answer for the same number of
cars depending on how fast they are. That's the whole point.

HOW MANY BUCKETS, AND WHY
-------------------------
Pressure: 6 buckets (kept the same edges as 1D so we can A/B on apples).
  pressure_edges = [-0.5, -0.2, 0.0, 0.2, 0.5]

Regime: 3 buckets. We chose:
  regime_edges = [-0.3, +0.3]
    bucket "calm"      regime ≤ -0.3
    bucket "normal"    -0.3 < regime ≤ +0.3
    bucket "elevated"  regime > +0.3

Why these edges? On Apr-21 data, p20≈-0.5, p50≈-0.07, p80≈+0.3. So
[-0.3, +0.3] roughly splits ticks into bottom 20% / middle 60% / top
20%. Each bucket gets enough data to learn from. (Apr-28 data is
shifted; see "expected behavior" below.)

Total cells: 6 × 3 = 18.

Data per cell on average: 38,977 / 18 ≈ 2,165 ticks. 1D was ~6,500
per bucket. So each cell has 1/3 the data of 1D. Less data per cell
= noisier learning per cell.

WHAT THIS COSTS
---------------
1. Sample size per cell shrinks 3× → noisier weights.
2. Some corner cells will be very sparse. Heavy buy + spike regime is
   rare; might get hundreds of ticks not thousands. Weights barely
   move from uniform.
3. Same total data, more parameters to fit. Classic statistical
   tradeoff.

WHAT WE EXPECT TO SEE
---------------------
Best case ("the thesis works for 2D too"):
  Some (pressure, regime) combinations have a different best action
  than the marginal pressure bucket. For example: heavy-buy + spike
  might prefer AGGRESSIVE even more than heavy-buy + calm. The 2D
  policy learns these. Total loss drops below 1D bucketed by some %.

Middle case ("regime is informative but undersampled"):
  2D is competitive with 1D but not significantly better. Saved by the
  cells where regime helps, dragged down by the sparse cells.

Worst case ("regime is decorative for this loss"):
  2D is WORSE than 1D. The regime signal didn't carry useful info for
  the slippage / adverse-move loss; cell-level noise dominates.

Note on Apr-28 data
-------------------
Regime spiked to +82.9 on this session (§18). Anything with regime > 0.3
falls in the "elevated" bucket. So that bucket is over-populated by the
spike — most "elevated" ticks are during a few high-vol bursts, not
spread evenly across the session. If those bursts had unusual best
actions, the elevated bucket will learn them. If those bursts were
chaotic (no consistent best action), the elevated bucket will just
have noisy weights.

THIS IS A FORK IN THE ROAD
--------------------------
At λ=0 (PURE SLIPPAGE — what you'd see on a broker invoice as
"$ paid above mid"), 2D BEATS 1D by 15%.

At any λ ≥ 0.1 (combined slippage + adverse-move risk), 2D LOSES by
~7% across the board.

WHY THE SPLIT — SIMPLE EXPLANATION
----------------------------------
Look at the action mix. 2D learned to take MORE PASSIVE (+2.4pp) and
LESS WAIT (-1.4pp). PASSIVE earns the spread when it fills (saves
slippage). But passive fills are toxic — they execute right before
unfavorable price moves on average (sanity §7). So:

  Slippage:     2D fills more passively   → SAVES money on spread.
                Apr-28 session: $6 saved per run (15%).
  Adverse:      Passive fills are toxic   → COSTS money on the next move.
                Apr-28 session: +1,048 in extra adverse_move per run.

At λ=0 only the first matters. At λ=0.1 the adverse cost is
0.1 × 1,048 = $104.8, which is way more than the $6.44 slippage saving.
So the combined cost goes UP.

In one sentence: 2D got MORE clever about WHEN to fill, but the extra
fills came with toxicity that the loss function (at λ=0.1) penalizes
more than the spread saving rewards.

WHY DIDN'T 1D HAVE THIS PROBLEM
-------------------------------
1D buckets on pressure only. In each pressure bucket, Hedge had ~6.5k
ticks to settle on the right action. The right action in heavy-buy is
AGGRESSIVE (no toxicity — you take liquidity, you don't post). The
right action in heavy-sell is WAIT (no fill, no slippage, no adverse).
1D was already near-optimal at avoiding toxic PASSIVE fills.

When we add the regime split, each cell has ~2.1k ticks. Hedge converges
slower per cell. In particular, the "near-zero pressure × any regime"
cells are where PASSIVE has the strongest competition — and 2D's smaller
sample size in those cells means it didn't down-weight PASSIVE as
aggressively. Result: 2D ends up posting more limit orders in the
neutral-pressure cells, which is exactly where toxic flow lives.

WHAT THIS DOES AND DOESN'T MEAN
-------------------------------
DOES mean:
  - "More state dimensions = better" is NOT free. Sample-size-per-cell
    matters as much as conditioning information.
  - On THIS data, with THIS loss (λ=0.1), 1D pressure-only is a better
    policy than 6×3 pressure×regime.
  - For pure execution-quality (slippage), 2D still wins. If the user
    of this system cares only about spread paid (e.g. a market-maker
    that hedges adverse risk separately), 2D is the right answer.

DOES NOT mean:
  - 2D "doesn't work." It works fine; it's a different operating point
    on the slippage / adverse tradeoff. The loss function at λ=0.1
    chose 1D. A different λ chooses differently.
  - regime is useless. Regime DID change behaviour (more PASSIVE in
    calm cells, less in spike cells). The change just wasn't
    profitable at λ=0.1 with 18 cells × 38k ticks of data.
  - The thesis is broken. The thesis says "state-conditioned > marginal";
    1D bucketed wins by 29% (§15) vs marginal still holds. The thesis
    was never "more states monotonically better."

LEVERS WORTH PULLING NEXT (NOT YET DONE)
----------------------------------------
1. Quantile-based regime_edges instead of fixed [-0.3, +0.3]. The Apr-28
   regime distribution is fat-tailed (max 82.9 from a calibration window
   that maxed at 4.1). Quantile edges would put each bucket near 1/3 of
   the data instead of letting the spike dominate the "elevated" bucket.

2. Recompute obs_center/obs_scale on Apr-20/21 + Apr-28 union so the
   standardization reflects both regimes. Currently it's calibrated only
   on Apr-21, which is why Apr-28 spikes look unusually large.

3. Coarser regime split: 2 buckets instead of 3 (just regime_edges=[0]).
   More data per cell, simpler policy. Possibly Pareto-better than 3.

4. Higher η (0.2-0.3) for 2D specifically. With less data per cell, a
   bigger learning rate may help converge before the run ends. Risk:
   overshoots in spike cells.

5. More data. A second 10h window won't fix sample-size-per-cell at the
   margin; we'd need 5-10× the data. Worth it long-term but not the
   first thing to try.

6. Different loss. Per (§9), λ=0.1 was chosen so PASSIVE is penalized
   for adverse selection but not crushed. λ=0.05 would give 2D's "more
   PASSIVE" stance more credit. λ=0.2 would punish it harder. The
   research question is whether the BEST 2D is better than the BEST 1D
   under each λ — so this lever is the "honest" one if we want to know
   if 2D has a regime where it dominates.

REPORTING TAKEAWAY
------------------
Two policies, same data, same Kalman, same execution model. They make
different tradeoffs:
  1D is better at COMBINED COST (slippage + adverse penalty).
  2D is better at PURE SLIPPAGE.
On a research project asking "given conditions, how to act?" the answer
is "depends on what you're optimizing." Both policies are using the
Kalman state — they're just using it to land on different points along
the spread-vs-toxicity frontier. That itself is a publishable finding.

WHAT HAPPENED — IN PLAIN LANGUAGE
=================================

The two policies are NOT making the same choices. 2D is taking more
PASSIVE actions (+2.3pp average) and fewer WAITs and AGGRESSIVEs. That's
the whole story behind every number in the tables.

Why does posting more PASSIVE limit orders matter?

1. PASSIVE earns the spread when it fills.
   In our market spread is about $0.01 — half-spread is $0.005.
   Every passive fill saves about $0.005 vs going AGGRESSIVE.
   With ~6,000 passive fills per run, that's ~$30 of slippage saved.
   2D fills ~700 more times than 1D and saves ~$6 more on net spread.

2. PASSIVE fills are TOXIC.
   "Toxic" means: when your limit gets filled, the price tends to move
   AGAINST you right after. Why? Because most of the time your passive
   order sits in the book waiting. The flow that crosses your limit and
   fills it is, on average, the flow that has a reason to push the
   price in that direction. So passive fills cluster right BEFORE
   unfavorable moves. We saw this in §7 — adverse_move on passive was
   2× the AGGRESSIVE one.

3. The combined loss = slippage + λ * adverse_move.
   The trade-off is "save spread by being patient, lose money on
   bad-luck-after-fill." At λ=0 only the spread savings count and 2D
   wins. At λ=0.1 the adverse term gets ten cents on the dollar, and
   2D's extra ~1,000 adverse units cost 0.1 * 1,000 = ~$100 — way more
   than the $6 spread saving.

So 2D is being "smarter at filling" but it picks up MORE toxic flow as
a side effect. At any λ above ~0.005 the toxicity bites harder than
the savings reward.

Why is 2D doing this?
---------------------
2D has 18 (pressure × regime) cells. 1D has 6 (pressure-only) cells.
38,977 ticks divided by 18 ≈ 2,165 ticks per cell. Some corner cells
are actually empty (heavy-buy AND heavy-sell extremes simultaneously).

Hedge needs many updates per cell to converge to a clean preference.
With only ~2,165 ticks per cell, the neutral-pressure cells in 2D
don't push down PASSIVE as hard as the same neutral-pressure pressure
bucket does in 1D (where it has ~6,500 ticks). So 2D ends up with a
slightly higher PASSIVE weight in the cells where toxicity lives.

Same data, more parameters to fit, less data per parameter, slower
convergence per parameter, and the slow-converging parameters happen
to favor an action that LOOKS good in slippage terms but has a hidden
cost. Classic statistical trade.

Did the regime split do ANYTHING useful?
----------------------------------------
Yes — at λ=0 it saved a real $6.37 vs 1D, with std $3.66 / sqrt(10)
≈ 1.16, so the slippage-only win is paired-significant too. The regime
information is real; it just happens to point the policy in a direction
that hurts at the loss the project currently optimizes.

If you were running a market-maker that hedged adverse risk separately
on a different timescale, 2D would be the better policy. If you're
optimizing the combined cost like we are here, 1D wins.

Headline interpretation
-----------------------
1. The §15 thesis "state-conditioned > marginal" still holds. 1D
   bucketed beats marginal by 29% (§15) AND beats 2D by 6% (§21).
2. "More state dimensions = monotonically better" is FALSE. 2D loses
   to 1D at the project's chosen λ even though it uses strictly more
   information from the Kalman filter.
3. The right framing is "find the policy at the right point on the
   sample-size-vs-conditioning curve for your loss." 1D pressure-only
   is that point on this data at λ=0.1.
4. The result is robust: 10/10 seeds agree, paired t = +17.73.

IN PLAIN LANGUAGE — WHAT HAPPENED AND WHY
=========================================

The two policies still make slightly different decisions. 2D takes a
bit more passive (16.77% vs 14.90%) and a bit less wait/aggressive. Same
direction as Tuesday. Same magnitude per tick.

Why does this matter?
  - PASSIVE earns the spread when it fills. Apr-30 spread was about
    $0.01, half-spread $0.005. ~13,500 passive fills on 1D, ~10,650 on
    1D — wait that's reversed — 2D fills more often (54.17% vs 53.06%
    fill rate). Across 63k ticks at λ=0 (slippage only) 2D saves $7.68.

  - PASSIVE fills are TOXIC. The flow that crosses your limit is the
    flow that has a reason to push the price against you. So passive
    fills cluster right before unfavorable moves. Apr-30 confirms this
    again: 2D's passive-heavy mix collected +1,608 raw units of extra
    adverse-move across the session. At λ=0.1 that's +$160.8 of
    additional cost. Way more than the $7.68 spread saving.

  - Net: 2D loses by $153.14 of combined cost per session at λ=0.1.

Why doesn't more state help?
  Apr-28 hypothesis was "2D's cells are too thin." Apr-30 makes the
  inner cells fat (2k-12k ticks each) — and 2D STILL loses. So thin
  cells were not the main driver. The remaining explanation: the
  regime signal IS informative for fill quality, but the resulting
  decision (more passive) is misaligned with our combined-cost
  objective. The pressure dimension already captures the "act
  asymmetrically based on imbalance" signal; the regime split adds
  fine-grained tuning that pulls policy toward more passives, which
  hurts at λ ≥ ~0.005.

If we wanted 2D to win at our λ, we'd need a regime signal that
correlates with FUTURE adverse-move risk, not just spread-fill
opportunity. Different research project.

24. ADVERSE-TARGETED 2D — TRYING TO PICK THE REGIME OFF THE FLOOR (2026-04-30)
================================================================================

WHY THIS EXPERIMENT EXISTS
==========================
§21 and §23 both showed the same thing on different days: the 2D
bucketed policy (pressure × Kalman regime) takes more passive fills,
saves a little slippage, but eats a lot more adverse_move and loses on
combined cost.

The diagnosis from §23: the Kalman regime axis is informative — but it
predicts FILL PROBABILITY, not ADVERSE-MOVE RISK. It tells the policy
"hey there are gaps to walk through" instead of "hey, walking now will
get you hit." So 2D used true information to make the wrong decision
for our cost.

§24 is the test of the obvious next idea: REPLACE the regime axis with
a signal designed to predict adverse risk specifically. Three candidate
signals (each computed inline from raw features over a rolling 60s
window):

  vol_delta     vol_30s_now − vol_30s_(now−60s)
                positive ⇒ volatility is *rising* (instead of "high")
  ofi_window    sum of ofi_l1 over the trailing 60s
                positive ⇒ sustained net buy pressure
                negative ⇒ sustained net sell pressure
  spread_delta  spread_abs_now − spread_abs_(now−60s)
                positive ⇒ spread is widening (a "things-getting-weird"
                signal)

The wager: if any of these correlates with adverse_move-after-fill
(rather than fill probability), then a 2D split on (pressure, signal)
might actually beat 1D on combined cost.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
1. src/features/adverse_signals.py
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Three small stateful classes for rolling-window second-axis signals.

Public interface (all three classes share it):
    sig = SignalClass(window_sec=60.0)
    value = sig.update(ts_ms, scalar_input)

Each one keeps a deque of (ts_ms, value) tuples, trims old entries
older than `now − window_sec`, and returns:
  - RollingVolDelta:    current_value − oldest_inside_window
  - RollingSpreadDelta: same as VolDelta but on spread_abs
  - RollingOFINetFlow:  running SUM of values inside the window

Warmup: until the buffer covers a full window (typically the first 60s
of a session) update() returns 0.0 — a neutral signal that buckets
treat as "no information yet."

A small registry maps the signal name to (class, raw_feature_to_read,
human_label):
    SIGNAL_REGISTRY = {
        "vol_delta":    (RollingVolDelta,    "vol_30s",    "vol_30s rising"),
        "ofi_window":   (RollingOFINetFlow,  "ofi_l1",     "60s net OFI"),
        "spread_delta": (RollingSpreadDelta, "spread_abs", "spread widening"),
    }

This file does NOT modify the FeaturePipeline or its parquet schema.
Signals are computed inline by the runner / A/B harness from raw feature
columns, so the canonical features parquet stays stable and the existing
1D / 2D scripts keep producing the same numbers.

How it differs from the existing src/features/volatility.py:
  RollingVolatility (existing) computes log-return realized volatility
  at three nested windows (1s, 5s, 30s) from mid-price ticks. Output is
  always a magnitude (>=0).
  The §24 signals are SIGNED differences over a single window, designed
  to capture *direction of change* rather than *level of volatility*.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
2. src/policy/hedge_adverse.py
----------------------------------------------------------------------
A NEW policy class plus a NEW context dataclass.

Why a new file (instead of repurposing BucketedHedge2DPolicy):
  BucketedHedge2DPolicy is wired to read `ctx.regime` from PolicyContext.
  That field semantically means "the Kalman filter's regime estimate."
  Reusing it for an unrelated rolling-window signal would be misleading
  AND would couple two unrelated experiments through a single field.
  Cleaner: introduce parallel types whose names are honest.

Two new types:

  class AdverseContext:
      ts_ms:           int
      market_pressure: float   # primary axis (same as before)
      secondary:       float   # the adverse-targeted signal (NOT regime)

  class BucketedHedgeAdversePolicy:
      def __init__(
          self,
          pressure_edges:  List[float],     # primary axis edges
          secondary_edges: List[float],     # NEW axis edges (per signal)
          learning_rate:   float,
          initial_weight:  float = 1.0,
          secondary_label: str   = "secondary",
          seed:            Optional[int] = None,
      ): ...

      def select(ctx: AdverseContext) -> PolicyDecision: ...
      def update(action: Action, loss: float) -> None:    ...
      def weights() -> Dict[Action, float]:               ...
      def bucket_summary() -> List[Dict]:                 ...

Mathematically identical to BucketedHedge2DPolicy: classical Hedge per
cell, multiplicative weights update, no shared state across cells.
The ONLY differences from BucketedHedge2DPolicy:
  - takes constructor args directly (no PolicyConfig wrapping)
  - second axis is `secondary` (not `regime`)
  - bucket_summary() emits 's_range' (not 'r_range') and includes the
    s_label so logs identify which signal was bucketed on

Reuses HedgePolicy.PolicyDecision verbatim — the output schema does
not change, so anything reading the per-tick decision log keeps working.

3. scripts/6_simulate_and_update_adverse.py
----------------------------------------------------------------------
A sibling of scripts/6_simulate_and_update.py and
scripts/6_simulate_and_update_2d.py. Same:
  - session detection (5s time-gap split, identical logic)
  - Kalman filter
  - simulator + loss
  - --session N / --list-sessions / --seed S CLI flags

What's different:
  - new --signal {vol_delta, ofi_window, spread_delta} flag (required)
    selects which adverse signal to use as the second axis
  - new --window-sec flag (default 60.0)
  - new --list-signals flag
  - replay loop is INLINE (does NOT call src/execution/updater.run)
    because updater.run only knows about PolicyContext, not
    AdverseContext. The inline loop is otherwise identical:
       Kalman → secondary_signal.update() → AdverseContext →
       policy.select → simulate_fill → compute_loss → policy.update
  - writes to a SEPARATE execution log dir
       data/derived/execution_log_adverse/run=<UTC-stamp>/
    so it does NOT collide with the canonical 1D/2D execution log
  - the per-tick log carries TWO extra fields:
       secondary       — the actual signal value bucketed on
       signal_name     — which signal generated `secondary`
    so post-hoc analysis can correlate signal value with adverse_move
    realized at the same tick

Default secondary-axis bucket edges are calibrated from pooled p33/p66
across the three real sessions on disk:
       vol_delta    [-1.1e-5, +9.0e-6]   3 buckets
       ofi_window   [-2.0,    +1.9]      3 buckets
       spread_delta [0.0]                2 buckets (more on this below)

Why spread_delta uses a single edge: the rolling 60s spread_delta is
≈ 0 for >99% of ticks in this market (BTCUSDT spread is almost always
exactly 1 tick = $0.01; rare widening events are sparse outliers).
Quantile-based edges degenerate (p33 = p66 = 0.0). A single edge at 0.0
asks the binary question "did the spread widen at all in the last 60s?"

IN PLAIN LANGUAGE — WHAT JUST HAPPENED
======================================

We tried THREE different "second axes" beyond pressure. The hypothesis
was: maybe the §21/§23 result wasn't "more dimensions hurts" but "the
Kalman regime axis is the wrong second dimension." If we picked one
that's actually about adverse risk instead of fill probability, the 2D
policy might win.

Two of three signals (vol_delta, spread_delta) FAILED in the same way
as the Kalman regime: more passive fills, more adverse hits, net loss.
Same toxic-flow story. So those two are off the table.

ONE signal (ofi_window) SHOWED MIXED EVIDENCE:
  - On the two smaller sessions (Apr-21, Apr-28, ~38k ticks each), it
    behaves like the others — saves a little slippage, eats a little
    extra adverse, loses on net at λ=0.1.
  - On the bigger session (Apr-30, ~64k ticks), it strictly dominates.
    Saves BOTH slippage and adverse-move. Wins 8/10 seeds. Wins at
    every λ from 0 to 0.5.

So we DID pick something off the floor — but only on one session, and
only with ~40 minutes more data than the smaller sessions had.

Why would the same signal flip from "loses by a little" to "wins
strictly" with more data?
  - Hypothesis A — sample size: 60s of 1s ticks is a long window. With
    only 38k ticks, the policy has fewer chances to accumulate
    statistically meaningful evidence per (pressure × ofi_window) cell.
    With 64k ticks the cells get enough data to learn the right pattern.
    If true, Apr-21/28 would win too if they had 64k ticks.
  - Hypothesis B — market character: Apr-30 had a distinct character
    (longer span, different volatility profile, different price range).
    The (pressure × cumulative_ofi) split might be informative
    specifically when there are multiple multi-hour trends in one
    session — which Apr-30 had and Apr-21/28 did not.
  - Hypothesis C — luck: 8/10 wins with |t|=2.37 is at the edge of
    "publishable significant." A second 64k+ session might or might
    not reproduce it.

WE CANNOT DISAMBIGUATE A vs B vs C with the data we have. The
straightforward way to find out is one more clean overnight session of
≥ 17h, then re-run the §24 A/B on it.

vol_delta and spread_delta are NOT the right axes for this project.
The mechanism in both is the same as the Kalman regime: they predict
when fills are easy, not when fills are toxic. They join the regime
field on the "informative for the wrong question" pile.


HEADLINE INTERPRETATION
=======================
1. The §15 thesis "1D state-conditioned > marginal" still stands.
   1D is still the winner across 3 sessions and now 4 different
   competitors (marginal, 2D regime, vol_delta, spread_delta).
2. ofi_window is the FIRST 2D variant to ever beat 1D on combined cost
   in any single session. The win is small (~0.4%, |t|=2.37) and only
   happens on the largest session, but the mechanism is qualitatively
   different from every previous 2D attempt: it saves both slippage
   AND adverse_move on Apr-30 instead of trading one for the other.
3. The §23 hypothesis "the Kalman regime axis predicts fill
   probability, not adverse risk" is now empirically grounded:
   - vol_delta and spread_delta have the same failure mode (fill-quality
     signal, fail at adverse).
   - ofi_window has a partially different failure mode (sometimes works,
     sometimes doesn't). Suggests the sustained net flow over a minute
     captures something about *direction of the next move* that the
     other two signals don't.
4. We do not yet have enough evidence to say "ofi_window 2D > 1D in
   general." We have evidence for "ofi_window 2D > 1D on Apr-30
   specifically." That is qualitatively new.


WHAT TO DO NEXT
===============
1. ✅ Keep 1D as the production default. Nothing here moves us off 1D.

2. ✅ Run another long (>15h) overnight session and re-run the §24 A/B
   on it. This is the cheapest way to test hypotheses A vs B vs C from
   above. If ofi_window wins on a second long session at |t| ≥ 2, that
   is real generalization. If it loses, the Apr-30 win was either
   luck or session-character-specific.

3. ⚠ Consider a 6×2 (instead of 6×3) ofi_window split. With binary
   "net buying / net selling" instead of trinary "selling / balanced /
   buying", each cell gets 50% more data. Cheap to try (one config
   change). Plausibly reduces noise on the smaller sessions where
   ofi_window currently loses.

4. ⚠ Try slightly higher η for ofi_window specifically (0.15 or 0.2).
   The signal is positive on Apr-30 with default η=0.1; faster learning

WHAT THIS EXPERIMENT IS, IN PLAIN WORDS
---------------------------------------
THE SANDBOX RULE.  At every tick, the agent has been told to BUY 1 unit
of BTC. Not "decide whether to buy". The buying is forced. The only
question is HOW to buy:

  WAIT       → don't send an order this tick. Skip and look again
               in 100 ms. Cheap if price is about to fall, costly if
               it's about to climb away from you.
  PASSIVE    → post a limit buy at the current best bid. Fills only if
               a seller comes down to our price next tick. When it
               fills, we save the half-spread (good). When it fills
               BECAUSE the market is dropping, we just bought right
               before a fall (toxic).
  AGGRESSIVE → cross the spread, take the best ask, fill guaranteed,
               pay the half-spread as instant slippage.

Every tick we lose money in two ways: SLIPPAGE (the $ we pay above
mid when we fill) and ADVERSE_MOVE (the $ the mid moved against us
right after we filled — i.e. we bought just before a drop). Total
cost per tick = slippage + λ * adverse_move with λ = 0.1 (a regret
multiplier set in §9 to make passive's toxicity hurt enough that the
policy can't just pick passive forever to game the slippage metric).

THE FOUR POLICIES WE COMPARE.
  baseline (1D)  Hedge weights split by current "market pressure" only.
                 6 buckets along pressure ∈ [-∞,-0.5,-0.2,0,+0.2,+0.5,+∞].
                 This is the §15 winner — beats marginal by +29% loss.
  A vol_delta    2D: pressure × (vol_30s_now − vol_30s_60s_ago).
                 Reads "is volatility rising in the last minute?"
  B ofi_window   2D: pressure × (sum of ofi_l1 over the last 60s).
                 Reads "has there been sustained net buy/sell pressure
                 over the last minute?"  — this is the only signal that
                 has ever beaten 1D at λ=0.1 in this project (§24, S3).
  C spread_delta 2D: pressure × (spread_now − spread_60s_ago).
                 Reads "is the bid/ask spread widening?" — meant to
                 catch liquidity drying up before adverse moves.

WHAT WE ARE TRYING TO PROVE.  §24 found that B beat 1D on the Apr-30
session 3 (8/10 seeds, |t|=2.37, only ofi_window achieved this — A
and C lost on every seed). Three explanations were possible:

  Hypothesis A — SAMPLE SIZE.   B needs ~64k+ ticks per session to
                                learn cleanly. 38k is below threshold.
  Hypothesis B — MARKET CHARACTER.  Apr-30 had multi-hour trends;
                                    the cumulative-OFI signal is only
                                    informative in trending markets.
  Hypothesis C — LUCK.          |t|=2.37 is at the edge of the usual
                                significance bar; could be noise.

Decision rule (set in advance — see §25 PLANNED block, now superseded):
  - B wins on session 7 with |t| ≥ 2.0  →  hypothesis A or B confirmed,

  - B loses with |t| ≥ 2.0              →  hypothesis C (luck) wins.
  - |t| < 2.0                            →  inconclusive, need a 3rd
                                            long session.

26. APPENDIX TO §25 — CAVEMAN-BRAIN EXPLANATION (2026-05-01)
================================================================================

This block is the same §25 result, but in plain words. Same numbers, no

THE WHOLE EXPERIMENT IN ONE LINE
--------------------------------
"1 vs A/B/C" is just "bucketed by ONE thing (pressure) vs bucketed by
TWO things (pressure + one extra axis). Pick any extra axis you want."

  1 = pressure                                  → 6 buckets
  A = pressure × (volatility rising in last 60s) → 6 × 3 buckets
  B = pressure × (net buy/sell flow last 60s)    → 6 × 3 buckets
  C = pressure × (spread widening last 60s)      → 6 × 3 buckets

Same data, same Hedge algorithm, same buy-1-BTC-every-tick rule. The
ONLY difference is what the second axis is. We are asking: does adding
a second axis make better decisions, and if so, which axis?


WHAT IS "LOSS" IN PLAIN WORDS
-----------------------------
We are forced to buy 1 BTC every tick. Two bad things can happen each
time:

  1. SLIPPAGE — the cash we pay above the fair mid-price to actually
     get the trade filled. Like the supermarket sells milk at $4.50
     but the corner store only has it for $5. The extra $0.50 is
     slippage. Real money, gone right now.

  2. ADVERSE MOVE — the price drops right AFTER we buy. We didn't
     lose cash out of pocket, but we definitely could have waited and
     paid less. Like buying milk at $5 then seeing it on sale for $4
     ten seconds later. That $1 is regret, not cash, but it still hurts
     the same way over thousands of trades.

  Total loss = slippage + 0.1 × adverse_move

The 0.1 (called λ) is a knob that says "regret hurts, but only one-tenth
as much as cash slippage". Without that knob the agent would learn to
wait passively forever and just sit there catching falling knives. The
0.1 forces it to take some bumps to dodge worse bumps.

So if you see "loss = 2868" on session 7, that means: about $105 of
real out-of-pocket slippage + $2,763 worth of regret about prices that
dropped right after we bought.


THE NUMBERS, SCALED TO REAL MONEY
---------------------------------
Session 7 = 21 hours straight. ~75,885 buy attempts, ~41,000 actually
filled. At $77,000 per BTC, that's about $3.2 BILLION of buying done in
this one session.

Against that giant pile of trades:

  strategy        cash slippage    regret      combined loss
  1D (default)    $105.56         $27,632      2868.74     ← winner
  A vol_delta     $97.17          $29,372      3034.38
  B ofi_window    $98.98          $27,675      2866.44     ← tied with 1D
  C spread_delta  $102.17         $28,616      2963.78

Per individual fill:
  - 1D pays about $0.0026 of slippage per fill (a quarter of a cent).
  - 1D suffers about $0.67 of regret per fill (price moved against us
    right after we bought).

So regret is roughly 250× larger per fill than slippage. That's WHY
adverse_move dominates the total loss number, even with the 0.1
multiplier knocking it down. If you see 1D's loss is ~2868, almost all
of that is regret, not actual cash out the door.


WHO WON, AND WHY THE DATA SAYS SO
---------------------------------
The winner is **1D bucketed (pressure only)** — the simple version we
already had. Not by being amazing. By being un-beaten.

  challenger A (volatility rising)        — tried 4 sessions × 10 seeds
                                            against 1D. Lost ALL 40
                                            comparisons. Big margins.
                                            Dead.

  challenger B (60s net buy/sell flow)    — tried 4 sessions × 10 seeds
                                            against 1D. Won 16/40.
                                            Won decisively only on Apr-30
                                            (8/10 seeds, |t|=2.37). On
                                            the new, BIGGER session 7
                                            it tied (5/10, |t|=0.23).
                                            Coin flip. Not a real win.

  challenger C (spread widening)          — tried 4 sessions × 10 seeds
                                            against 1D. Lost ALL 40
                                            comparisons. Big margins.
                                            Dead.

When we say "|t| = 0.23", that's a noise check. Rule of thumb:

  |t| < 1.0  → totally inside the noise. Same as flipping a coin.
  |t| ≈ 2.0  → starting to look real, but still 1-in-20 to be luck.
  |t| > 3.0  → almost certainly real.
  |t| > 10.0 → no doubt at all, the difference is real.

The losses for A and C had |t| values of 12-35. Real, no doubt. The
"win" for B on session 7 had |t|=0.23. Pure noise. The "win" for B on
Apr-30 had |t|=2.37 — borderline real, and now we know it didn't repeat.

So 1D wins by default: nothing has clearly beaten it across multiple
sessions yet.


WHY DID A AND C MAKE THINGS WORSE?
----------------------------------
Both signals (volatility rising, spread widening) basically tell the
agent "things are getting weird right now". The agent's reaction is to
become MORE PASSIVE — sit on a limit bid, hope a seller comes to us,
save the spread.

This is wrong. When things are getting weird, sellers come to your
passive bid because **the price is dropping**. You "saved the spread"
by buying at the bid right before the price fell another $20.

  BEFORE (1D, S7):           54.13% of ticks filled, $105 slip, $27,632 adv
  AFTER (A, S7):             55.38% of ticks filled, $97 slip,  $29,372 adv

A saved $8 in slippage by filling 1.25 percentage points more often.
But those extra fills cost $1,740 more in adverse moves. Net: A is $165
worse on combined loss.

Same story for C, just less extreme.

The signals are not useless — they really do detect "things are getting
weird". The agent just reacts to that signal in the wrong direction.
Maybe the right reaction is the OPPOSITE: when vol is rising or spread
is widening, get MORE aggressive (or wait), not more passive. We
haven't tested that yet.


WHAT THIS WOULD LOOK LIKE IN THE REAL WORLD
-------------------------------------------
If you ran 1D on a real Binance API with $1M of buying capacity per day:

  - Expected slippage cost: about $33 per million traded per day.
    (BPS: 0.033 — basically free, in execution-cost terms.)
  - Expected regret: somewhat larger but harder to pin down because it
    depends on what you would have done with the money you "wasted" by
    buying just before drops.

If you ran A or C instead, you'd pay an extra $30-ish per million
traded per day in regret-equivalent. Doesn't sound like much, but at
institutional volume that compounds into the difference between a
profitable execution desk and one that's barely breaking even.

If you ran B instead of 1D, you'd see no measurable improvement on
real money — the difference is below the noise of normal trading.
You'd just have a more complex system to maintain (3× as many
weight tables, a 60-second rolling buffer to keep current) for
nothing.


WHAT WE CAN START TO ASSUME (NOT PROVE — JUST BELIEVE A LITTLE)
---------------------------------------------------------------
Each of these is a hypothesis the data lets us start trusting. Treat
them as starting points for the NEXT experiment, not as facts.

  1. AT 100 ms BTC, KNOWING THE PRESSURE IS ENOUGH.
     Every attempt to add a second axis (Kalman regime, vol_delta,
     spread_delta, ofi_window) has either lost outright or failed to
     clearly win. The simplest model is winning. Adding state splits
     the data thinner without adding enough signal.

  2. "THINGS ARE GETTING WEIRD" SIGNALS PUSH THE AGENT THE WRONG WAY.
     vol_delta and spread_delta both make the agent more passive in
     volatile / widening-spread moments. Both LOSE. Real signal,
     wrong reaction. Worth testing the inverted version (be more
     aggressive when things are getting weird).

  3. ofi_window IS REAL BUT NARROW.
     It's the only signal that has ever beaten 1D. It won once
     (Apr-30) and tied once (May-1). There's likely a market regime
     ("sustained one-sided flow") where it would win clearly, but
     we haven't isolated it. Next move is regime-CONDITIONAL, not
     swap-the-policy.

  4. ONE-SESSION WINS ARE NOT WINS.
     Apr-30 ofi_window looked solid (|t|=2.37). We almost promoted
     it. Session 7 cooled it back down. Going forward, no policy
     change should be considered a real win without ≥ 2 independent
     sessions at |t| ≥ 2.0. Cheap rule that would have caught this.

  5. THE BIG GAINS, IF THEY EXIST, ARE NOT IN STATE BUCKETING.
     Three different attempts to enrich state hit a wall. The next
     real improvement probably comes from a DIFFERENT SHAPE of
     decision (variable order size, multi-tick planning, queue-
     position modeling) — not from a smarter version of "split the
     ticks into more buckets and learn weights per bucket".

27.1 LIVE-MODE BOOK CORRUPTION — diagnosed and fixed
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Symptom Damián saw running scripts/10_live_gui.py in Live mode:
  - Spread column showing values like -100, -84, -112 (impossible
    in a real book — best_ask is always ≥ best_bid).
  - Slippage on AGGRESSIVE rows showing -50, -42, -56 (also
    impossible — AGG buys at best_ask, slippage = best_ask − mid
    must be POSITIVE in a healthy book).
  - The savings curve trending strongly NEGATIVE (-$1,992 in
    ~130 ticks).

Root cause: live_source.py was violating the Binance snapshot+stream
sync protocol — the same pattern that caused the §1 bug. Original
code did:
    snapshot = fetch()           ← snapshot first
    open WS                      ← WS second
    apply WS events
That order leaves a hole between the snapshot and the first WS
event during which Binance has already advanced the book, and the
deltas that subsequently arrive apply to a stale state. The local
book diverges from reality, eventually best_bid creeps above
best_ask, and every downstream computation goes sideways.

Fix landed in two iterations:

  Iteration 1 — naive sequential reorder
    1. open WS
    2. buffer WS events for ~2.5s
    3. fetch snapshot
    4. find bridge in buffer
    Failed because Binance REST often returns AFTER WS has
    advanced — snapshot.last_update_id ends up AHEAD of every
    buffered WS event ("snapshot ahead of buffer"). Code retried
    forever.

  Iteration 2 — concurrent state machine (current code)
    1. open WS, kick off snapshot fetch on a separate task at the
       same time
    2. read WS events with 0.3s timeout, accumulate into buffer
    3. when snapshot lands, classify buffer relative to snap_uid
       into one of four states:
         'found'  — bridge event found, apply snapshot + bridge +
                    tail
         'ahead'  — all buffered events end before snap_uid →
                    keep reading WS, bridge will come
         'behind' — first buffered event starts past snap_uid+1
                    → snapshot is stale, refetch
         'gap'    — there's a post-snapshot event but it doesn't
                    bridge → refetch snapshot
    4. absolute 20s timeout on the whole seeding step
    5. on detected sequence gap during live processing, force a
       reseed by closing the WS

Defensive guard added: every emitted feature is checked for
crossed-book pathology (best_ask > best_bid, mid in [bid, ask],
spread > 0). Anything failing those checks is dropped with a
warning rather than fed into Kalman.

Files:
  src/live/live_source.py       — full rewrite of _run_async / _seed
  scripts/10_live_gui.py        — unchanged

Verification: after the fix, the live source emits a clean log line
"LiveSource SEEDED — snap_uid=... bridge=[U=..., u=...]" once per
successful seed, then transitions to live processing. No more
"snapshot ahead of buffer" loops.

Replay mode was unaffected by this bug — it consumes the offline
parquet which was generated by the proper book_builder pipeline
that follows the §5.1 self-heal logic correctly.

27.6 INTERACTIVE TERMINAL WALKTHROUGH
----------------------------------------------------------------------

  scripts/_walkthrough_terminal.py

What it is: a slow, ENTER-to-advance pipeline visualization that
walks through every block of the diagram for one tick at a time,
in Spanish, with pretty-printed Unicode matrices (⎡ ⎣ ⎤ ⎦) and
per-cell annotations explaining what each entry of each matrix
means.

Block sequence per tick (each block waits for ENTER):
  [BLOQUE 1]  Fila del parquet — ts_ms, bid/ask/mid/spread,
               depth_imb, ofi_l1, vol_30s, next mid
  [BLOQUE 2]  Estandarización — z_raw → (z_raw − center) × scale
               → z_norm, with per-component arithmetic shown
  [BLOQUE 3a] Kalman PREDICT — x_prior, P_prior, F, Q each shown
               with their description and per-cell meaning;
               x_pred = F·x_prior step by step; P_pred = F·P·Fᵀ + Q
               with intermediate (F·P), (F·P·Fᵀ), and final result
  [BLOQUE 3b] Kalman UPDATE — H, R introduced with per-cell
               annotations; innovation y = z_norm − H·x_pred;
               innov covariance S; gain K = P·Hᵀ·S⁻¹ (with
               intermediate P·Hᵀ and S⁻¹ shown); posterior
               x_post = x_pred + K·y; posterior P_post
  [BLOQUE 4]  Bucket lookup — pressure value, edges, the 6 buckets
               listed with their semantic meaning, "← AQUÍ" marker
               on the chosen bucket
  [BLOQUE 5a] Frozen weights — distribution for chosen bucket with
               Unicode bar visualization
  [BLOQUE 5b] Action sampling — cumulative slices, u draw, action
  [BLOQUE 6]  Simulator — fill outcome with adaptive text
  [BLOQUE 7]  Loss computation — slippage, adverse, λ·adverse, total
  [BLOQUE 8]  Hedge update (illustrative) — exp(-η·L) factor, before/
               after weights, renormalization, per-action delta
  [RESUMEN]   tick loss, cum loss, baseline cum, savings vs always-AGG

The matrix annotation format (per-cell meaning):

        H[i,j] = 1 si la feature i 'mira' directamente al estado j; 0 si no.

27.7 CONCEPTUAL Q&A CAPTURED IN THE CONVERSATION
----------------------------------------------------------------------

These came up in the working session and are worth preserving even
though the answers live in the docs above. The defense Q&A and
math_explained docs cover the core; this is a quick index of
question→answer-location for fast lookup.

  Q: Are these "trades" other people buying BTC?
     → No. Each row is OUR agent's simulated decision against the
       recorded order book. Market data is real, action and fill
       are simulated. (RESULTS_REPORT.txt has the analogy of the
       two pretend traders.)

  Q: How can we "know how much money we save"?
     → It's a counterfactual: same data, two pretend traders, one
       runs marginal/AGG-baseline, the other runs our policy.
       Subtract their clipboard totals. (math_explained.md Part 1
       and "savings number" appendix.)

  Q: Are those numbers "real"?
     → Real in the sense that the data is real and the math is
       correct. Not real in the sense that fees, queue position,
       latency are not modeled. The savings is a fair clipboard
       comparison, not a P&L forecast. (defense_qa Q44/Q45.)

  Q: Is the Kalman framework specific to this problem, or generic?
     → The FRAMEWORK (predict + update, the matrices F/H/Q/R/K/P)
       is universal. The DIMENSIONS and VALUES are problem-
       specific. For a braking system you'd have 3 states
       (pos/vel/decel), 3-dim observation (GPS/wheel/accel), F
       encoding kinematics with cross-terms, etc. For us the 2D
       state (pressure, regime) and 3D observation (depth, ofi,
       vol) come from the Bouchaud transient/permanent
       decomposition.

  Q: Are we using online learning anywhere?
     → Yes during offline TRAINING (the Hedge update rule applied
       to recorded data is exactly online learning). No in the
       GUI deployment — we use frozen weights for reproducibility
       and deterministic demo. The Kalman filter does online
       INFERENCE (state updates each tick) but not online learning
       (its parameters F/H/Q/R never change).

  Q: Would online learning be beneficial?
     → For real deployment yes — adapts to regime shifts. But it
       has tradeoffs (loss of reproducibility, no guarantees on
       live performance, drift accumulation). Industry pattern is
       a frozen production policy + a shadow learner + periodic
       promotion. Crucially: online learning would NOT escape the
       toxicity barrier — that's mechanistic, not adaptive.

  Q: What is the toxicity barrier in plain words?
     → Every signal we tried as a 2nd axis of state conditioning
       (Kalman regime, vol_delta, ofi_window, spread_delta) is
       really a fill-easy detector. When it fires the Hedge update
       reweights toward PASSIVE in that cell. But fills in the
       fill-easy moments are toxic — they're the ones where the
       counterparty has a directional reason to cross your bid.
       Slippage savings get more than reversed by adverse cost.
       Identical mechanism across 4 independent signals → it's
       structural to bucketed-Hedge at λ=0.1, not signal-specific.

  Q: Do we measure regret? What's the regret bound?
     → We don't measure regret as a reported metric. We DO use
       Hedge, which is a no-regret algorithm — its update rule
       achieves the bound by construction. The bound is
       O(√(T·log K)) — for our T=75k, K=3, that's ≤ 287 units of
       regret total over a 21h session, ~0.4 cents per decision.
       It's a worst-case ceiling under any sequence of losses,
       even adversarial.

  Q: What is S in the Kalman update? Why "innovation"?
     → y is the residual z − H·x_pred — "innovation" because it's
       the NEW information the tick brings (the part not predicted).
       Term comes from signal-processing innovation sequences.
       S = H·P_pred·Hᵀ + R is the EXPECTED variance of y if the
       model is right. Two pieces: H·P·Hᵀ (predict uncertainty
       projected to obs space) + R (sensor noise itself). K uses
       S⁻¹ as inverse-variance scaling so the gain is small when
       S is big (lots of expected noise → don't react) and large
       when S is small (precise observation → trust it).

  Q: In the Kalman trace plot, why is regime mostly negative?
     → Because (a) the standardization centers regime at the
       calibration window's MEAN of vol_30s, and vol_30s has a
       fat positive tail — the mean is dragged up by rare spikes
       and the median sits below the mean. (b) Regime < 0 just
       means "calmer than the calibration baseline (Apr 20-21)".
       It's expected behavior given the data distribution, not
       a bug. Doesn't matter for the 1D winning policy because
       1D doesn't bucket on regime.

  Q: How do I justify this thesis to a hedge fund?
     → reasons.txt has the full narrative. Short version: poker
       solvers (CFR family) and trading execution (Hedge family)
       are the same mathematical structure — sequential decision
       making under partial information with discrete action sets
       and a probability-distribution-per-state output. Pivot to
       microstructure was driven by data availability. The 3-action
       sandbox is the canonical decomposition of any execution
       problem. The toolbox (Kalman + Hedge) is exactly what HFT
       desks use day-to-day.
